1.1 · Five definitions, thirty seconds each
Sparse Mixture of Experts (MoE)
Store many specialist networks, use a few per token. K3 stores 896 experts and fires 16, so each token pays the arithmetic of a mid-size model while the system holds 2.8T parameters of capacity. That is the whole trick behind "huge but affordable." The catch: memory and networking still pay for all 896, which is why serving stays industrial even though compute per token is modest.
Kimi Delta Attention (KDA)
Moonshot's linear attention: a fixed-size memory that erases stale content and writes new content, instead of storing every past token. Cost per token stays flat whether the conversation is 10 tokens or 1 million. This is the mechanism that lets K3 sell 1M-token context without a premium price, and the reason the $0.30 cached tier can exist. The trade: a fixed memory cannot guarantee exact recall, so some full-attention layers remain.
Attention Residuals (AttnRes)
Normally each layer receives one running sum of everything the layers before it produced. AttnRes lets a layer look back and pull the specific earlier outputs it needs. Published result: a model with AttnRes matched one trained with 1.25x more compute. If that holds at K3's scale, it is a direct cut to training cost, worth hundreds of millions of dollars at frontier budgets.
Quantization and QAT
Quantization stores numbers in fewer bits; quantization-aware training (QAT) trains the model in those small formats from the start so quality holds. K3 ships 4-bit native. Economically: 4-bit weights roughly halve the machines needed to hold the model, which widens who can serve it, including on export-constrained hardware.
Expert parallelism
The 896 experts live on different chips, and every token travels over the network to its 16 chosen experts, at every expert layer. This is where MoE's savings send the bill: less arithmetic per token, more interconnect traffic between chips. It is why an "efficient" giant model still drives demand for premium networking.
1.2 · In one sentence

Kimi K3 is the open-weight attack on the closed frontier at full force: claimed 2.5x cheaper to build, top-five in measured intelligence, free to copy from 27 July. It is not cheaper to run, and part of the build savings may be borrowed from the closed models it undercuts.

Executive summary

1.4Five things that matter

The argument in order: what the machine is, who is forced to move, why serving is the moat, what the price tells you, and the open question.

  • The efficiency is a stack, not one algorithm. Kimi Delta Attention (KDA) cuts memory across the sequence. Five separate savings, and every one must hold in production or they never reach the bill. Only the first two have published evidence, and only at small scale.
  • The game: closed labs move first, the serving layer collects. K3 cuts the closed labs' quality lead from months to days on coding and agent work. Moonshot's own return is position, not revenue: free weights buy it the ecosystem, and its paid API holds only until hosts undercut it. Application-layer startups and enterprises win twice: cheaper access, and from 27 July the weights themselves, to fine-tune on proprietary data, distill into fast product models, and run inside their own clouds where the data never leaves. So 27 July is a handoff to the big hosts: Azure, AWS, CoreWeave, Nebius, Together, Fireworks. 1.5 · The mechanisms, briefly
  • Attention: most layers run Kimi Delta Attention (KDA), a fixed-size memory that erases and rewrites itself; a few keep gated Multi-head Latent Attention (MLA) for exact recall.
  • Depth: Attention Residuals (AttnRes) let deep layers pull specific earlier outputs instead of one blended stream.
  • Capacity: a Mixture of Experts with 896 specialists, 16 active per token.
  • Precision: trained from the supervised fine-tuning stage in the 4-bit and 8-bit formats it ships in (QAT, MXFP4/MXFP8).
  • Evidence: KDA and AttnRes were published with code inside Kimi Linear, Moonshot's 48-billion-parameter research model. That proves they work at 48B; nothing yet proves them at 2.8T. SiTU, Per-Head Muon, and Quantile Balancing are names without published detail. Full detail: ② Technical Mechanism.
  • AI that builds AI: the launch's most novel demonstrations are recursive. K3 wrote MiniTriton, a compact GPU compiler that matches or beats Triton and torch.compile on supported benchmarks, and designed, optimized, and verified a chip to serve a nano model of its own architecture in one 48-hour autonomous run. Demonstrations, not production, and that distinction carries the whole story: ② 2.4.
1.6 · The economics, briefly
  • Cheap where context repeats. Coding agents resend the same repository every step; Moonshot reports over 90% cache hits there, priced at $0.30 per million cached input tokens against $3.00 fresh.
  • Ordinary where tokens are generated. Output costs $15.00 per million at a measured 36.9 tokens per second, and on the full Artificial Analysis suite K3's cost per completed task, $0.94, sits next to GPT-5.6 Sol's $1.04. K3 is cheap only when the workload replays context. On everything else it is priced like the closed frontier.
  • The gains are in building, not running. The claimed 2.5x is training efficiency. The arithmetic of generating a token is untouched.
  • 27 July hands this cost structure to every capable host. Who captures the margin is the open question. Full argument: ③ Economics. To interrogate any claim, press Glyph it! above the tabs, then highlight it.

Concept cards

2.1The vocabulary, with context

Twelve cards, closed by default. Open what you need; each gives the definition, where the idea comes from, what it does inside K3, and the source to go deeper.

KV cache

The stored key and value representations that let a model reuse earlier context instead of recomputing it.

Every transformer since 2017 pays this bill. It grows with each token and sits in expensive GPU memory for every active user, which is why context length is priced.

In K3: the object the whole design shrinks and reuses.

Economics: GPU memory is the scarcest serving resource; whoever shrinks the cache serves more users per machine, and context pricing follows.

MLA paper (DeepSeek-V2)Kimi Linear

Multi-head Latent Attention (MLA)

DeepSeek's 2024 compression: each token's cache entry becomes a small latent vector, roughly a 90% reduction against standard attention.

MLA displaced grouped-query attention (GQA) as the efficiency standard and was adopted by Kimi K2, GLM-5, and others. Compression, not removal: the cache still grows with context.

In K3: the baseline it departs from. The retained full-attention layers are gated MLA.

Economics: the compression that made long context affordable industry-wide in 2024-25; serving cost models still assume it.

DeepSeek-V2 paper

Linear attention

A family of attention replacements holding a fixed-size state, so cost per token stays constant at any context length.

The known weakness: a finite state cannot guarantee exact recall of an arbitrary early token. Modern designs compensate with better update rules and hybrid layouts.

In K3: the mechanism class behind roughly three of four layers.

Economics: a fixed state means long-context price stops scaling with length, the direct threat to premium context pricing.

Gated DeltaNet

The delta rule

The update discipline of modern linear attention: erase the stale part of memory before writing the new part.

Without it, recurrent memories either decay everything uniformly or drown in accumulation. NVIDIA's Gated DeltaNet (2024) combined the delta rule with a learned forget gate; Qwen3-Next ships it.

In K3: the ancestor rule KDA refines.

Economics: no direct cost effect on its own, but every downstream saving depends on this update staying stable.

Gated DeltaNet paper

Kimi Delta Attention (KDA)

Moonshot's refinement of the gated delta rule: the forget gate operates per channel, so each dimension of the state decays at its own learned rate.

Published October 2025 in the Kimi Linear paper with code and a 48B-total model. Measured there: up to 75% cache reduction and up to 6x decode throughput at 1M-token context. Those are 48B results, not K3 results.

In K3: the core sequence-memory mechanism.

Economics: the mechanism behind flat-cost 1M context and the $0.30 cached tier; if it holds at 2.8T, long context stops being a premium product.

Kimi Linear paperCode

Hybrid attention

Interleaving linear-attention layers with periodic full-attention layers: cheap long-range memory plus precise retrieval where it pays.

Kimi Linear used a 3:1 ratio after testing alternatives: 7:1 hurt validation quality, 1:1 wasted inference. K3's exact pattern awaits the configuration files.

In K3: the layout that makes a finite state safe to trust.

Economics: the insurance that caps quality loss from the cheap layers, which is what makes the savings sellable.

Kimi Linear paper

Attention Residuals (AttnRes)

Attention aimed at the model's own depth: each layer queries earlier layers' outputs and pulls in what it needs, instead of receiving one fixed sum of everything.

The standard residual stream adds every prior layer with unit weight, which the AttnRes paper argues bloats hidden states and dilutes contributions. Block AttnRes retrieves block summaries to keep overhead under 2%. Published March 2026: matched a baseline trained with 1.25x more compute, at 48B scale.

In K3: the depth-efficiency mechanism; its 2.8T contribution is the report's biggest open number.

Economics: 1.25x less training compute at frontier budgets is hundreds of millions of dollars per run, if it scales.

AttnRes paperCode

Mixture of Experts (MoE) and expert parallelism

Store many specialist networks, activate few per token. Expert parallelism spreads them across accelerators and moves each token to its chosen experts over the network fabric.

Arithmetic becomes selective; storage, routing, and communication do not. At K3's scale the network is part of the model.

In K3: 896 routed experts, 16 active, about 1.8%.

Economics: moves cost from arithmetic to interconnect, which is the semis complex's highest-margin product.

K3 disclosure

Quantile Balancing

Expert allocation derived from the router's own score distribution rather than from an added balancing penalty in the training loss.

The standard penalty approach forces even expert usage but distorts what the model learns. Routing instability is the classic failure mode of very sparse training runs.

In K3: the claimed stability method behind 1.8% activation at 2.8T. No published experiments isolate its contribution yet.

Economics: routing collapse wastes entire training runs; stability is what turns 1.8% activation from a risk into an asset.

K3 disclosure

Quantization, QAT, MXFP4/MXFP8

Quantization represents weights and activations in fewer bits, reducing memory traffic and arithmetic cost. Quantization-aware training (QAT) trains the model in the target formats instead of compressing a finished model.

MXFP4 and MXFP8 are 4-bit and 8-bit microscaling floating-point formats with hardware support across current accelerators. A trillion-scale model is a data-movement problem before it is an arithmetic problem.

In K3: QAT from the supervised fine-tuning (SFT) stage onward; the shipped model is natively 4-bit.

Economics: 4-bit weights roughly halve the machines needed to hold the model, widening who can serve it, including constrained hardware.

K3 disclosure

Prefill, decode, prefix cache

Prefill processes the input prompt in parallel. Decode generates output one token at a time. A prefix cache stores the processed state of repeated context so it is never prefilled twice.

Agent workloads resend nearly identical context every step. Cache hit rate, not raw speed, dominates their cost. Moonshot's Mooncake serving system separates prefill and decode into different resource pools.

In K3: over 90% reported hits on coding traffic, and a 10x cached-input price discount built on it.

Economics: agent workloads live on hit rate, not model size; this is what the $0.30 tier prices.

Mooncake paper

Named but under-documented

Three K3 components are disclosed by name with little detail.

SiTU, a Sigmoid Tanh Unit, is a new activation function intended to improve attention selectivity; its formula and placement are unpublished. Per-Head Muon extends the Muon optimizer to update attention heads independently. Gated MLA adds selectivity gates to the retained full-attention layers.

In K3: no published experiments isolate the contribution of any of the three.

Economics: unverifiable components are launch risk; if the technical report skips them, the efficiency story rests on trust.

K3 disclosure

The efficiency stack

2.3Six engineering layers, one unverified scaling claim

2.3.1 · Sequence: KDA plus gated MLA

Full attention, including Multi-head Latent Attention (MLA), retains a token-level history that grows with the sequence. Kimi Delta Attention (KDA) instead maintains an editable finite state: read, write, erase as tokens arrive. Periodic gated MLA layers retain precise retrieval. The Kimi Linear paper measured up to 75% cache reduction and up to 6x decode throughput at 1M-token context, on a 48B-total model. Those magnitudes are background evidence, not K3 results.

2.3.2 · Depth: Attention Residuals

A standard transformer hands each layer one running sum of everything the earlier layers produced. Attention Residuals replace that with selection: a layer looks back and pulls the specific earlier outputs it needs. The published result, from Kimi Linear, Moonshot's 48-billion-parameter research model: with AttnRes the model matched a version trained with 1.25x more compute, for under 2% extra cost. K3 says it uses AttnRes. No one has measured what it contributes at 2.8T, and the 2.5x claim does not separate it out.

2.3.3 · Experts: 16 of 896, and the coordination bill

Selective activation cuts per-token arithmetic to roughly a mid-size model's while storing 2.8T parameters of capacity. The hidden cost is coordination: router quality, load balance, chip placement, and the network traffic of tokens crossing between chips. Quantile Balancing sets expert allocation from the router's own score distribution instead of a penalty in the loss. Moonshot also reports engineering choices that keep the GPUs from ever waiting on the host CPU. The training run finished and the model serves traffic: real but indirect evidence that the stability methods work.

2.3.4 · Precision: trained for the formats it ships in

Quantization-aware training (QAT) from the supervised fine-tuning stage onward, MXFP4 weights, MXFP8 activations. A trillion-scale model is a data-movement problem before it is an arithmetic problem, and low-bit formats cut the movement. One illustrative lower bound: 2.8T parameters at four bits is 1.4TB before scales, metadata, activations, caches, and replication. The released files will set the real footprint.

2.3.5 · Serving: reuse and phase separation

Moonshot reports over 90% prefix-cache hits on its own coding traffic and promises its KDA-compatible cache implementation to vLLM alongside the weights. Mooncake, Moonshot's published serving architecture, separates prefill and decode into different resource pools. A workload-specific hit rate is not a universal hit rate, and none of this discloses margins.

2.3.6 · Scale: the 2.5x claim is unverified

The package plus refined data recipes yields, per Moonshot, roughly 2.5x better overall scaling efficiency than Kimi K2. Until the report defines the metric, compute range, and token counts, this is a directional author claim. It may mean more capability per training FLOP. It does not disclose total FLOPs, dollars, or energy, and it does not say where the data gains come from.

Recursive demonstrations

2.4AI that builds AI

The launch's most novel material is not an efficiency number. It is K3 improving the tools AI runs on, two layers below itself.

  • The compiler: MiniTriton. K3 developed a compact Triton-like GPU compiler: its own tile-level intermediate representation on top of MLIR, optimization passes, and a PTX code-generation pipeline. Moonshot reports it performs on par with or better than Triton and torch.compile across supported roofline benchmarks (tests scored against the hardware's theoretical speed limit), beating Triton on certain workloads. Translation: the model wrote the software that decides how fast models run on GPUs.
  • The chip. K3 designed a chip to serve a nano model built on its own architecture. In a single 48-hour autonomous run it built, optimized, and verified the design using open-source electronic design automation (EDA) tools on the Nangate 45nm library. A 45nm research library is decades behind production silicon; the point is the autonomous loop, not the chip.
  • The caveats: both are author-reported demonstrations. Nothing says MiniTriton runs in Moonshot's production stack, and nothing says either result fed back into training K3 itself.
  • Why it matters: this is the early shape of recursive self-improvement, AI improving the toolchain AI depends on: compilers, kernels, silicon. If a future model closes this loop in production, training-efficiency gains stop being one-off engineering and start compounding, and that would move every number in ③ Economics.

Game theory

3.1The game: six players, one forced move

Six players: the closed labs, Moonshot and the open labs, the hosts, NVIDIA, the app builders, and enterprise buyers. Each has one obvious rational move. Put the moves together and you know what happens next without predicting anything.

Closed labs. They cannot ignore this, and they cannot price-match a rival that just raised prices. So: cut the cheap tiers K3 hosting will commoditize, hold frontier pricing, and sell what K3 lacks, speed and terseness. The first defensive move may already be on the board: on 18 July, two days after K3 launched, Anthropic reversed its plan to pull Claude Fable 5 from subscription plans and made it permanent. Anthropic cited demand and capacity; the timing sits two days after K3. Next: tighten API access, because every served token is potential training data for the next K-series. That is rational with or without proof of the February allegation.

Moonshot. It gave the model away and raised prices 3 to 4x. That is not confusion, that is the strategy. The weights release is irreversible, so hosts can invest in serving K3 knowing Moonshot cannot take it back. The raised list prices the window before those hosts arrive, and two things can be true about it at once: it takes margin while the window lasts, and it reflects what a 2.8-trillion-parameter model actually costs to serve. Either way, Moonshot is buying position, not profit.

The hosts: Azure, AWS, CoreWeave, Nebius, Together, Fireworks. Move fast or lose the land grab: R1 hosting appeared in days. Serving K3 well is hard, and that difficulty is the product. The best caching engineering wins the $0.30 cached tier profitably. The trap: underprice the interconnect bill and you win traffic at negative margin.

NVIDIA. Sell the fabric, the high-speed network that connects the chips, and skip the panic. K3 cuts no inference arithmetic, and its cache savings come back as interconnect demand. January 2025 already ran this experiment: down 17% in a day, new highs by June. The real threat in the launch post is the unnamed alternative vendor's general-purpose GPU, and the answer to that is software lock-in, not price.

App builders. Cursor already trains its Composer models on open Kimi checkpoints, and after 27 July every application company can do the same. Owning frontier weights is a different business than renting an API: fine-tune the frontier on your own data, distill it into small fast models shaped to your product, pin the exact version forever with no deprecation risk. None of that is possible through a closed API, and it removes the dependency that mattered most: no single closed vendor controls their margin anymore.

Enterprise buyers. Dual-source and measure cost per completed task, not per token. K3 is verbose, so its $15.00 output can cost more per task than a terser closed model, while cached coding traffic is an order of magnitude cheaper. Route by workload. Weights also change what an enterprise can do at all: run frontier intelligence inside its own cloud where regulated data never leaves, audit the exact model it depends on, and keep a version no vendor can retire. And the mere threat of switching now disciplines every closed-lab renewal: buyers won this release without lifting a finger.

Where it lands. Moonshot ships, hosts undercut, closed labs cut the low end and defend the top, NVIDIA sells more fabric, app builders and enterprises play both sides. The oldest rule of commoditization is doing the work here: your margin is my opportunity, aimed at model IP. Three pressure points inside that: closed-lab pricing on commodity tiers only ratchets down, and it re-ratchets at every credible open release. Distillation never fully stops, because closing the tap completely would ruin the closed labs' own product; expect partial leakage plus the occasional public accusation, which is exactly what February was. And hosting is a race where showing up late means not showing up.

What breaks it: 27 July slips or ships thin. No major host inside two or three weeks. A closed lab cuts frontier prices across the board. Enforcement visibly degrades the next K-series. A scaled K3 deployment on the alternative-vendor chip. Watch those five events; most other coverage is noise.

The frontier

3.2What is demonstrated, what is merely possible

Demonstrated. The open-weight frontier now ships within days of the closed frontier, with architecture disclosure the closed labs no longer provide. Hybrid linear attention has a flagship committed to it at 2.8T scale. Low-bit training reached 3T-class scale in the open. Independent measurement places K3 among the strongest models available days after launch.

Possible, not established. If KDA-class attention holds quality at scale, million-token context stops being a premium feature. If AttnRes's compute advantage holds at 2.8T, depth-wise retrieval becomes a fourth design axis beside attention, routing, and precision. A launch post establishes neither.

The asymmetry

3.3Cheaper to build, not cheaper to run

One approximation carries this section: training a model costs about 6 x N x D floating-point operations (N active parameters, D training tokens); generating a token costs about 2 x N. The two terms fund different industries, and K3's gains land on the first.

  • Training term: where every K3 gain lives. The 2.5x efficiency claim is a training claim. AttnRes's published 1.25x is a training claim. KDA and caching cut memory and long-context overhead, not the arithmetic of producing a token.
  • Inference term: untouched, and the measurements agree. 36.9 output tokens per second, and $0.94 per completed task on the Artificial Analysis suite, next to GPT-5.6 Sol's $1.04. K3 wins on building cost, not on running cost.
  • The price list, read term by term. $0.30 cached input is work avoided, not work done cheaper. $15.00 output carries no discount, and the whole list sits 3 to 4x above K2.6: margin while the window lasts, or the true cost of serving 2.8T, or both. Third-party prices after 27 July decide.
  • The question this puts to the frontier labs. If open labs keep cutting the cost of building capability while serving costs fall more slowly, closed training capex buys less lead. Watch three things: closed release cadence, whether closed prices hold where open parity is proven, and whether the next open flagships repeat the same pattern.
The distillation question

3.4Was K3 partly taught by the closed frontier?

  • The mechanism: training on a stronger model's outputs buys capability per training FLOP far beyond organic scaling, and does nothing for serving cost. That is K3's profile: an undecomposed 2.5x efficiency claim, top-five measured intelligence, no inference-side advantage against GPT-5.6 Sol or Claude Fable 5.
  • The accusation, on the record: on 23 February 2026 Anthropic publicly alleged that Moonshot, DeepSeek, and MiniMax ran coordinated distillation attacks on Claude: roughly 24,000 fraudulent accounts, more than 16 million exchanges, targeting reasoning, tool use, coding, and agentic behavior. Those are the workloads where K3 now leads.
  • The limits: the allegation predates K3 and does not establish what sits in its training data. Launch-week reception cut against the strong form: OpenAI's Dean Ball wrote "I don't think its performance can be explained away by distillation," and researchers noted the agentic results are hard to explain through distillation alone.
  • The stakes: if closed capability leaks into open models within months, the return on closed training capex decays on a clock. The counters are already visible: hidden reasoning traces, output rate limits, terms enforcement, faster shipping.
  • What settles it: the technical report's data disclosure, independent behavioral-similarity analyses once weights land, and whether K3's edge tracks exactly the workloads where the closed leaders excel.
  • The door swings both ways: after 27 July, K3 is the strongest open teacher available, and the R1 precedent says distilled students spread faster than the giant itself.
Four threads

3.5Where the economics actually connect

3.5.1 · Neocloud serving

K3 is the most hosting-friendly frontier release to date: open weights, 4-bit native, a vLLM prefix-cache path contributed by the lab, and an economics profile built on cache-heavy agent workloads, which is the product neoclouds sell. The serving arithmetic, ours:

  • It does not fit one machine. 2.8T parameters at 4 bits is about 1.4TB of weights. An 8-GPU H200 or B200 node carries roughly 1.1 to 1.5TB of high-bandwidth memory (HBM), so the weights alone saturate a node. K3 is multi-node from day one; Moonshot recommends 64 or more accelerators.
  • Wide expert parallelism sends the bill to the network. The 896 experts spread across many GPUs, and every token crosses the network to reach its 16 experts at every expert layer. The memory KDA saves comes back as interconnect traffic.
  • Serving quality separates the hosts, not GPU count. The best caching engineering wins the $0.30 cached tier profitably; underpricing the interconnect bill wins traffic at negative margin.
  • A floor exists below the premium tier. The launch's own hardware list: H200, H20, and an unnamed alternative vendor's GPU. Fast serving needs premium fabric; mere serving does not. Where hosting concentrates after 27 July settles it.

3.5.3 · Open against closed

  • The gap: Epoch AI measured open-to-closed at roughly four months before K3. On its chosen workloads K3 compresses it to days, with third-party confirmation.
  • The read: Nathan Lambert calls the release "a watershed moment because frontier open-weight models are now real" and ties it to Beijing's public commitment to open-source diffusion.
  • The tension: Lambert predicted in April that Chinese open labs would face funding pressure this year. Moonshot answered by raising list prices while giving the weights away: monetize like an incumbent, distribute like an insurgent.
  • The margin reading holds here: a lab that needs revenue, holding a brief monopoly on its own model, charges what the window allows.
  • The floor mechanism: every credible open flagship resets the price floor that closed per-token pricing must justify, workload by workload. K3 aimed at agentic coding, the largest app-layer spend.

3.5.4 · Hardware

The launch post's hardware breadth is the signal: NVIDIA H200 for the flagship case study, H20 for one benchmark branch, an unnamed alternative-vendor GPGPU alongside, and a 64-accelerator supernode recommendation with no vendor named. Read together with MXFP4 weights, the design intent is portability across the memory-rich tier of the market, inside and outside export constraints. The same breadth is why the SemiAnalysis demand argument and the diffusion argument can both be right: the premium tier serves K3 fastest, the constrained tier serves it at all.
